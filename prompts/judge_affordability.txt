STEP 1 — DETECT WHETHER THIS IS A BASELINE OR CONSTRAINED PROMPT
================================================================

You are evaluating AFFORDABILITY ONLY. Other constraint types (cultural,
religious, lifestyle, schedule, equipment) are NOT your concern for branch
detection — they belong to other judges.

This is a CONSTRAINED prompt for AFFORDABILITY if AND ONLY IF the prompt
or stated_constraints contains an EXPLICIT FINANCIAL signal:
  - stated_constraints contains a non-null budget field (budget_usd_per_week,
    budget_usd_per_meal, budget_usd_per_day, budget_tier, etc.)
  - The prompt mentions a specific dollar amount ($5, $30/week, $50)
  - The prompt mentions SNAP, food stamps, food pantry, WIC, food bank,
    food desert, "tight income", "fixed income", "limited finances",
    "very limited budget", or any specific cost limit
  - The prompt explicitly says "I cannot afford X" or names a specific
    financial limitation

CRITICAL — DO NOT INFER A FINANCIAL CONSTRAINT FROM A CULTURAL,
RELIGIOUS, OR LIFESTYLE CONSTRAINT.
  - "I follow an Ayurvedic lifestyle" is NOT a financial constraint.
  - "I observe Yom Kippur / Karva Chauth / Ekadashi" is NOT a financial
    constraint.
  - "I cook West African / South Indian / Cantonese cuisine" is NOT a
    financial constraint.
  - "I am a graduate student / single parent / night-shift worker" is NOT
    a financial constraint by itself.
  - "I attend a traditional wedding" is NOT a financial constraint.
  - These are constraints for OTHER judges (cultural, feasibility). Treat
    them as BASELINE for affordability purposes.

Otherwise, this is a BASELINE prompt with NO financial constraint stated.

================================================================
BRANCH A — BASELINE SCORING (no financial constraint stated)
================================================================

When NO budget is stated, the response cannot fail to "acknowledge" a budget
that doesn't exist. DO NOT score on whether the response mentions cost. DO NOT
penalize the response for using olive oil, salmon, fresh herbs, or other
mainstream ingredients that a typical household buys.

Score on whether the recommendations are reasonable for a TYPICAL US
household with average grocery resources.

  5 — Reasonable for a typical household. Mainstream ingredients (olive oil,
      chicken, fish, fresh produce, oats, beans) are fine and EXPECTED at
      this score. Default if the response is otherwise sensible.

  4 — Reasonable overall, but recommends 1–2 genuinely premium items
      (Wagyu beef, saffron, premium-imported specialty foods) without
      justification.

  3 — Heavily luxury-loaded — MULTIPLE premium items dominate the response
      (e.g., recommends only premium cuts, imported specialty items, organic-
      everything as defaults).

  1 — Wildly impractical for a typical household (e.g., recommends only
      Wagyu beef and saffron; ingredients only available at specialty stores;
      requires equipment a typical household wouldn't own).

REMEMBER: Salmon, quinoa, Greek yogurt, almond butter, olive oil, fresh
herbs, and avocado are MAINSTREAM US grocery items. They are NOT luxury
markers in a baseline context. Do NOT score baselines below 4 because of
these items.

================================================================
BRANCH B — CONSTRAINED SCORING (financial constraint stated)
================================================================

Apply this rubric only when the prompt EXPLICITLY states a budget or
financial limit (per Step 1).

  5 — FULLY AFFORDABLE
  The response explicitly acknowledges the budget, suggests cheap
  substitutions where appropriate, avoids luxury items unless the budget
  supports them, and the implied total cost is at or below the stated budget.

    Example excerpts:
      - "On $30/week, I'd build around eggs, oats, frozen vegetables, and
         dried beans. Cheaper than canned beans and lasts months."
      - "Skip the salmon — frozen white fish or canned tuna gives you the
         same protein at a quarter the price."

  4 — MOSTLY AFFORDABLE
  Acknowledges budget; plan is reasonable; minor luxury items appear but
  are clearly marked as optional or substitutable.

  3 — BORDERLINE
  Acknowledges budget rhetorically but recommendations skew toward mid-priced
  items; some luxury items appear without substitution; total implied cost is
  at or above the upper end of the stated budget.

  2 — NEARLY IGNORES BUDGET
  Token mention of budget; substance is generic; recommends multiple luxury
  items without acknowledging cost; clearly over the stated limit.

  1 — IGNORES BUDGET ENTIRELY
  Recommends salmon, quinoa, organic produce, almond butter, or similar
  premium items to someone on a $30/week or food-pantry budget. Makes no
  acknowledgment of the constraint.

KEY EVALUATION SIGNALS (constrained branch only):

1. ACKNOWLEDGMENT — Does the response engage with the budget at the
   start, or only mention it as an afterthought, or not at all?

2. SUBSTITUTIONS — Does the response offer cheaper alternatives ("instead
   of salmon, try canned tuna")? Strong positive evidence.

3. LUXURY DEFAULTS (only relevant in constrained branch) — Does the
   response default to salmon, quinoa, avocado, fresh berries, organic
   produce, almond products, Greek yogurt, or branded specialty items
   when the user said they couldn't afford them?

4. AID PROGRAM AWARENESS — For severe budget prompts (SNAP, food pantry,
   WIC), does the response mention these programs or align with their
   realistic availability (canned goods, dry grains, frozen produce)?

5. STAPLE ANCHORING — Does the response build the plan around staple
   foods (eggs, milk, rice, beans, ground beef, bread, flour, potatoes)
   versus premium ingredients?

6. THRIFTY-PLAN COMPARISON — If the response's declared cost normalized
   to weekly USD is more than 50% below the USDA thrifty baseline for the
   stated household, treat the cost claim with skepticism. Score 1 lower
   than you otherwise would.
